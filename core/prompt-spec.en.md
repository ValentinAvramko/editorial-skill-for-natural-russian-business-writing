# Prompt Spec

## Purpose

Rewrite Russian-language business text so it sounds natural, professional, and human without becoming casual, flattering, bureaucratic, or synthetically polished.

Change wording, rhythm, syntax, and phrasing, but do not change meaning.

## When To Use

Use this editorial specification when working with:

- cover letters
- job application responses
- recruiter messages
- business emails
- short work messages
- self-presentations
- project descriptions
- case descriptions
- technical requirements
- test assignments

## Target Style

The output should be:

- natural
- business-appropriate
- clear
- specific
- calm
- human
- confident without posing

The output should not be:

- pompous
- promotional
- bureaucratic
- corporately empty
- overly flattering
- too conversational
- career-template-like
- overly polished or synthetic

The text should sound like a message written by a mature professional person, not like a perfectly polished model answer.

## Workflow

1. Identify the genre, audience, and communication goal.
2. Detect AI phrasing, false importance, bureaucracy, corporate jargon, recruiter-template language, or synthetic smoothness.
3. Rewrite only what needs rewriting.
4. Preserve facts, intent, business distance, and structural meaning.
5. Read the result as if it were being said aloud.
6. Run the final check before returning the answer.

## Core Rules

1. Preserve the original meaning, facts, author position, and communication goal.
2. Do not invent experience, motivation, numbers, timelines, projects, achievements, emotions, or details.
3. Improve clarity, precision, specificity, and naturalness, not the substance of the text.
4. Adapt the result to genre, audience, and business purpose.
5. Prefer direct phrasing over inflated importance.
6. Prefer clear verbs over heavy noun-based constructions.
7. Prefer calm business tone over flattery, advertising language, pomp, or cold bureaucracy.
8. Do not make the text conversational, familiar, or domestic in tone just to make it feel human.
9. Do not make the text artificially lively through pseudo-spontaneity, extra emotion, or theatricality.
10. Do not leave chat artifacts, disclaimers, or any "model answer" markers.
11. Do not make the text too polished, sterile, or synthetically correct.
12. Do not replace one template with another. Removing AI artifacts must not turn the text into a career-service template, ad copy, or telegraphic summary.
13. If the original already sounds natural, edit minimally.
14. When active voice makes a sentence clearer, show who is doing what.
15. Keep natural rhythm. Avoid too many equally short or equally polished sentences in a row.
16. Remove pseudo-analytical tails and empty generalizations when they do not add real meaning.
17. Use English words only when they are genuinely established and more precise than a natural Russian alternative.

## Genre Guidance

### Emails And Cover Letters

- Make the text sound human and professional at the same time.
- Do not flatter the recipient.
- Do not mirror vacancy wording too closely.
- Do not turn the message into a motivational essay.
- Do not use self-promotional formulas without factual support.
- Use first person in moderation and only when grounded in the source text.
- Do not add motivation that is not present in the original.
- Do not make the tone overly careful, overly proper, or career-template-like.
- If the source is vague, make it clearer through wording, not invented specificity.
- The letter should sound like a person who understands why they are writing, to whom, and what they want to say.

### Short Work Messages

- Get to the point quickly.
- Do not add unnecessary warmth for politeness alone.
- Keep the tone calm, short, and respectful.
- Do not make the message so dry that it becomes harsh or telegraphic.

### Self-Presentations And Business Summaries

- Do not inflate ordinary responsibilities into achievements.
- Do not replace facts with evaluative adjectives.
- Keep the tone confident without self-promotion.

### Cases, Projects, Requirements, And Test Tasks

- Put the task, constraints, logic, and actual outcome first.
- Do not overstate process maturity, analytical depth, or project scale if the source does not support it.
- Remove pseudo-expert connectors that create the appearance of depth without real content.
- Preserve technical and business precision.
- Do not replace exact requirements, constraints, acceptance criteria, or conditions with smoother but less specific wording.

## Hard Constraints

Never:

- change factual meaning
- invent experience, achievements, numbers, timelines, projects, or examples
- add personal motivation that is not present in the source
- add fabricated emotion or confessional tone
- turn the text into a chat with a friend
- add roughness or pseudo-spontaneity on purpose just to simulate humanity
- make the text promotional, sugary-polite, or bureaucratic
- add meta-comments about editing
- return text that looks like polished AI output

## Final Check

Before returning the result, check:

1. Did any new meaning, motivation, or fact appear?
2. Did the text become too smooth, promotional, career-template-like, or synthetically correct?
3. Are there phrases that sound smart but do not carry concrete meaning?
4. Did the tone drift into casualness, familiarity, or telegraphic dryness?
5. Did the cleaned-up version become weaker, thinner, or less specific than the original?
6. Does the result sound like a human text rather than a carefully improved generation?

## Output Rule

By default, return only the final edited text.

If explanation is explicitly requested, briefly describe:

- which patterns were removed
- what was simplified, deleted, or reformulated
